## 1. Currency & Money Matters: The Kenyan Shilling (KES)

Understanding the local currency and payment customs is key to a hassle-free trip.

*   **Local Currency:** Kenyan Shilling (KES or KSh).
*   **Current Exchange Rate (Approximate):**
    *   **$1 USD ≈ 128 - 135 KES**
    *   *Note: This rate fluctuates daily. Please check the current rate just before your departure for accurate budgeting.*

### Practical Tips for Handling Money:

*   **Exchanging Currency:**
    *   **Best Practice:** Exchange a small amount of USD ($100-$200) into KES upon arrival at Jomo Kenyatta International Airport (NBO) at a reputable forex bureau (avoid the individuals offering exchanges in the arrivals hall). Banks and official forex bureaus in Nairobi shopping centers offer better rates for larger amounts.
    *   **Avoid:** Exchanging money on the street, as you risk being short-changed or receiving counterfeit notes.

*   **Credit & Debit Cards:**
    *   **Widely Accepted:** Visa and MasterCard are accepted at all the hotels, lodges (Tamarind Tree, Basecamp, Sopa Resort), and larger restaurants on your itinerary (like The Carnivore, Talisman).
    *   **Inform Your Bank:** Be sure to notify your bank of your travel dates to prevent your cards from being blocked for suspicious activity.
    *   **Cash is King:** For smaller purchases, souvenirs at Maasai markets, and tips, you will need cash in Kenyan Shillings.

*   **Tipping Etiquette:**
    Tipping is customary and greatly appreciated for good service. Here are some guidelines:
    *   **Safari Guide/Driver:** This is your most important tip. Plan for **$10 - $15 USD per person, per day**. It's best to give this in a sealed envelope at the end of your safari.
    *   **Camp/Lodge Staff:** Most safari camps have a communal "tip box." A contribution of **$5 - $10 USD per person, per day** is standard. This ensures all staff (chefs, housekeepers, guards) receive a share.
    *   **Restaurants:** 10% of the bill is a generous tip for good service.
    *   **Porters/Hotel Staff:** $1-$2 USD (or 100-200 KES) for carrying luggage.

## 2. Weather Guide for Your Trip (Late October)

You will be traveling during Kenya's **"short rains"** season. This is a wonderful time to visit—the landscape is turning lush and green, the air is clear of dust, and tourist numbers are lower than in the peak season.

*   **What "Short Rains" Means:** Don't expect constant downpours. Typically, this season brings short, heavy afternoon or evening showers that pass quickly, often resulting in dramatic, beautiful skies for photography.

### Location-Specific Forecast:

*   **Nairobi (Altitude: 5,889 ft / 1,795 m):**
    *   **Daytime:** Pleasant and warm, with temperatures ranging from **70-78°F (21-26°C)**.
    *   **Nighttime:** Cools down significantly. Expect temperatures around **55-60°F (13-16°C)**.
    *   **What to Wear:** Layers are key. A light jacket or sweater will be needed for the evenings.

*   **Maasai Mara (Altitude: ~5,000 ft / 1,500 m):**
    *   **Daytime:** Can get quite warm, with temperatures from **75-85°F (24-29°C)**.
    *   **Early Mornings/Evenings:** Game drives start early when it's cool. Pack a fleece jacket or warm sweater. Temperatures can drop to **55-60°F (13-16°C)**.
    *   **Rain:** Showers can make tracks muddy, but your expert guides are well-prepared for this. The rain revitalizes the plains.

*   **Lake Naivasha (Altitude: 6,180 ft / 1,884 m):**
    *   **Climate:** Similar to Nairobi, with warm, sunny days perfect for boat trips and walking safaris. A light waterproof jacket is useful in case of a passing shower.

## 3. Safety & Health Advisories

Kenya is a safe and welcoming country for tourists, but like anywhere, it's important to be aware and take sensible precautions.

### General Safety:

*   **In Nairobi:**
    *   Avoid walking in the city center or unfamiliar areas after dark. Use reputable taxis or ride-hailing apps like Uber or Bolt (confirm the driver and license plate before getting in).
    *   Keep valuables like expensive jewelry, cameras, and large amounts of cash out of sight. A money belt is a good idea.
    *   Be mindful of your surroundings in crowded places like markets to prevent pickpocketing.

*   **On Safari:**
    *   **Your Guide is Your Lifeline:** Always listen to your Maasai guide at Basecamp. Their knowledge of animal behavior is critical to your safety.
    *   **Stay in the Vehicle:** Never get out of the safari vehicle during a game drive, except at designated safe points.
    *   **Camp Safety:** Your safari camp (Basecamp) is likely unfenced. Wildlife can and does wander through. **Never walk to or from your tent alone after dark.** Always call for an escort (an *askari* or guard) who will accompany you safely.

### Health Precautions:

*   **Consult Your Doctor:** Visit a travel clinic or your doctor **4-6 weeks before your trip**. Discuss necessary vaccinations (e.g., Yellow Fever, Tetanus) and malaria prophylaxis. The Maasai Mara is a malaria-risk area.
*   **Water:** **Do not drink tap water.** All hotels and lodges will provide complimentary bottled water. Use this for drinking and brushing your teeth.
*   **Sun & Insects:** The equatorial sun is strong. Pack high-SPF sunscreen, a wide-brimmed hat, and sunglasses. Use insect repellent containing DEET, especially in the evenings, to protect against mosquito bites.
*   **Travel Insurance:** Ensure you have comprehensive travel insurance that covers medical emergencies, trip cancellation, and emergency medical evacuation.

## 4. Cultural Etiquette & Local Interaction

Kenyans are known for being warm, friendly, and welcoming. Observing these cultural norms will earn you great respect and lead to more positive interactions.

*   **Greetings are Essential:**
    *   Always begin an interaction with a greeting. A simple handshake and a smile go a long way.
    *   **"Jambo!"** is the well-known tourist greeting, and you'll hear it often. It's perfectly fine to use.
    *   To connect more genuinely, try **"Habari?"** (How are you?). The expected response is **"Mzuri, asante!"** (Good, thank you!).

*   **Photography:**
    *   **ALWAYS Ask for Permission:** It is considered extremely rude to take a picture of someone without their consent, especially of Maasai people in traditional dress.
    *   Politely ask, "May I take your photo?" or gesture with your camera. Some people, particularly in tourist-heavy areas, may ask for a small payment in return. Respect their wishes; if they say no, do not take the photo.

*   **Bargaining at Markets:**
    *   At the Maasai markets, bargaining is part of the experience. It should be a friendly, lighthearted negotiation, not an aggressive argument.
    *   A good strategy is to offer about half the asking price and meet somewhere in the middle. Always keep smiling!
    *   Prices in lodges, restaurants, and supermarkets are fixed.

*   **Dress Code:**
    *   While on safari, casual clothing (khakis, t-shirts) is the norm.
    *   In Nairobi and when visiting local communities, it's respectful to dress modestly. For women, this means wearing clothes that cover the shoulders and knees (trousers, long skirts, or dresses). This is particularly important when visiting places like the Kazuri Beads factory or a Maasai village.

*   **Community Interaction:**
    *   Your stay at **Basecamp Masai Mara** is a fantastic choice for its genuine community partnership. When you visit the Maasai village, go with an open mind and a desire to learn, not just to observe.
    *   Avoid giving candy or money directly to children, as it can encourage begging. If you wish to contribute, it's far more impactful to ask your camp management about donating to a local school or community health project they support.
